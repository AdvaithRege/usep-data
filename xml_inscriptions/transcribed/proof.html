commentary

It is unclear whether the fec (line 2) represents the common abbreviation for fecit or if the last two letters were cut off by a break in the stone. It seems to me that there is, in fact, room for both letters in the area that is broken and I have encoded them as if they were inscribed, but this is not quite certain.

I have printed carisimo (line 3) without correction because it is quite common to have what are "classically" geminated consonants replaced by single consonants (cf. ILS, vol. 3, p. 802; for carisimus, specifically, see CIL 6.20094, 6.21273, 6.29324) in the imperial period.

The -quae (line 5) actually stands in for the connective -que. The substitution of -ae for -e was common in the epigraphic corpus, especially with -que (cf. ILS 6167, 7220, 7925, 8192, 8225 etc.). Aeorum in line 6, likewise, stands in for eorum. However, the substitution of -ae to -e is not consistent throughout the inscription as we see posterisque in line 5-6.

The b that is inscribed in line 7 seems to be a mistake - we have no other instances of any word starting with b following heredem in the epigraphic corpus. It is most likely that the carver meant to inscribe an e for exterum following the standard formula ending funerary inscriptions: hoc monumentum heredem exterum non sequetur.

Interpuncts usually appear between words and not at line-ends, but there appears to be no interpunct between carisimo and suo (line 4) and liberquae and posterisque (line 5-6). The lettering is much bigger in the first four lines of the inscription than in the last three lines of the inscription.
